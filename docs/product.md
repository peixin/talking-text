# Product

## Origin

A lot of kids are learning English—online classes, offline classes, textbooks, workbooks. They spend hours, yet rarely get to actually *speak*. Language should accumulate the way chatting does: daily, casually, in a safe environment. Kids whose first language isn't English don't have that environment.

General-purpose AI chat products (like Doubao) can already talk to a child in English, but they come with **no boundaries**—they throw out words the child hasn't learned, grammar the child hasn't seen. The child doesn't understand, gets frustrated, and the "chat" turns into another test.

So we're doing something very simple:

> **Feed the child's textbook into the AI, and let the AI chat within that scope.**

Every word that comes up is a word the child has studied. Every sentence that reappears is one the child has seen before. Every time they open their mouth, they're speaking from inside what they already know.

But looping endlessly inside the known doesn't make the child grow. The linguist **Stephen Krashen** proposed the **i + 1** hypothesis: the most effective language input is *what you already know, plus a little bit*—familiar enough to feel safe, new enough to cause growth.

So we don't lock the child in a vocabulary prison—we run a **gradient** chat:

- **~90%** of the conversation uses words and patterns the child has learned—so they feel safe and dare to speak.
- **~10% (adjustable)** quietly mixes in words from the next unit, or same-level, same-topic vocabulary—just a tiptoe away.
- When a new word appears, the surrounding context must make its meaning clear—the way a toddler learns from their parents' speech, without a dictionary, purely from context.

Until they know it so deeply that they can walk out of the app and say it to Doubao, to a real person, to the world, with confidence—while their "known" quietly grows.

The goal isn't to teach the child new things. It's to—

> **Make what they've already learned truly theirs—and while that's happening, let new things quietly grow in.**

## Brand

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| English name | **Talking Text** |
| Chinese name | **字有天地** (*zì yǒu tiān dì* — "words contain worlds") |
| Slogan | **言出成界** (*yán chū chéng jiè* — "speak, and worlds form") |

**Talking Text** — Text is the subject; Talking is its state. Not a concatenation of two features, but "text that speaks." The static words on a textbook page come alive.

**字有天地** — "Within words, there are worlds." Four characters holding Wittgenstein: the limits of language are the limits of world. Words *are* the world. The more words you have, the larger your world.

**言出成界** — Four characters carrying two souls: **言出** (words spoken) is Li Bai's *embroidered mouth pouring forth*—action. **成界** (worlds formed) is Wittgenstein's *language is world*—result. Speak, and the world exists.

## Symbolism

### Wittgenstein: The limits of language are the limits of world

> **German original:** Die Grenzen meiner Sprache bedeuten die Grenzen meiner Welt.
> **English:** The limits of my language mean the limits of my world.
> — Ludwig Wittgenstein, *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*, 1921

In the *Tractatus*, Wittgenstein stated something plain and vertiginous: the range of what a person can say is the range of what they can think, perceive, inhabit. What cannot be said is not part of their world.

Apply this to a child learning English—how large is their English world? Exactly as large as the words and sentences they've studied.

Traditional English teaching keeps pushing the child *past* this boundary: unfamiliar words, unseen grammar, reading above their level. The child keeps hitting walls.

We do the opposite. **First, let the child walk every inch inside the boundary until it becomes their own.** Once the small world is solid, the boundary expands naturally.

### Li Bai & Yu Guangzhong: One breath from an embroidered mouth, and half the Tang Dynasty spills out

> Wine enters his heroic gut—
> seven parts ferment into moonlight,
> the remaining three howl out as sword-breath,
> **one breath from his embroidered mouth, and half the Tang Dynasty spills forth.**
> — Yu Guangzhong, *Searching for Li Bai*, 1980

This widely quoted line was not written *by* Li Bai—it was written *to* him, by the Taiwanese poet Yu Guangzhong over a thousand years later. In a single image, Yu captures Li Bai's soul: the wine he drank turns into moonlight and sword-breath, and when he opens his mouth, out pours half the Tang Dynasty itself.

Why half a dynasty? Because half a dynasty was *inside* him—its classics, its landscapes, its people, its music, its wine. Li Bai wasn't performing a world; he *was* that world. When he spoke, the world flowed out of him.

Apply this to a child. What's inside the child? Their textbook. Every word, every sentence they've studied. We want every time they open their mouth to feel like Li Bai's—**not fragments, but a whole small world of their own, pouring out**.

### The two thinkers, one product

Wittgenstein defines the **boundary**: what you can say is your world; what you cannot, is not.

Yu Guangzhong (speaking for Li Bai) defines the **center**: from the depths of what you've absorbed, what pours out is complete and grand.

Combine them, and you get our product philosophy:

> **Don't make the child hit the boundary. Let them speak from the center of their own world—and push the boundary outward, one inch at a time.**

Every lesson the child has studied is their own *half a Tang Dynasty*. Our job is to sit with them while they pour it out, again and again, until it is truly theirs—and while they pour, let new stars quietly join their dynasty.
